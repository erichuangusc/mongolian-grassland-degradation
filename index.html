Grassland Degradation on the Mongolian Plateau: Classification and Predictive Modeling

Mia Zhou, Cindy Zhou, Xinyang Xu, Eric Huang
University of Southern California
Paper Website

Mapping land degradation patterns using Sentinel-2 imagery and Dynamic World probabilities to predict bare soil expansion one year in advance.

Abstract

Grassland degradation on the Mongolian Plateau is accelerating under the combined pressure of climate change and human activities, turning large areas into persistent bare soil and major dust sources. Recent work has mapped long-term land degradation patterns using coarse resolution NDVI and land cover products, but has remained largely descriptive and retrospective. In this project, we frame the prediction of bare soil one year in advance as a supervised classification problem with a resolution of 10 m. For each pixel or 224×224 Sentinel-2 chip, we predict whether it will be bare or non- bare-dominated in year t+1, given satellite imagery and precipitation up to year t. We construct labels from normalized Dynamic World bare probabilities in the following years and fuse multi-season Sentinel-2 mosaics with annual precipitation aggregates from TerraClimate to form the inputs. The spectral information of Sentinel-2 is interpreted using the official band specifications of Sentinel Hub.

Overall, our findings show that (1) short-horizon desertification at 10 m resolution is meaningfully predictable from recent imagery and climate history, and (2) transformer models that have representation learning capabilities can substantially outperform traditional feature-engineered classifiers forn this task, especially in spatially heterogeneous desert margins.

Data & Study Regions

Study Regions

We focus on two sub-regions of the Mongolian Plateau that capture contrasting degradation regimes: Kubuqi, a dune field in Inner Mongolia characterized by extensive bare sand and engineered vegetation belts, and East MN, an eastern Mongolian grassland region where bare soil appears mainly in patches and along disturbance corridors.

Data Sources

For each region, we assemble a dataset at 10 m resolution consisting of three primary sources:

  • Sentinel-2 (L2A): We utilize six spectral bands with strong vegetation and soil sensitivity: Blue (B2), Green (B3), Red (B4), NIR (B8), SWIR1 (B11), and SWIR2 (B12).
  • Dynamic World: We use the V1 product to extract per-pixel "bare soil" probabilities, which serve as the basis for our ground-truth forecasting labels.
  • TerraClimate: To capture inter-annual moisture variability, we integrate 4 km gridded annual precipitation aggregates from 1958 to present.

Label Construction & Splitting

We define the forecasting target as a binary classification problem. Using Dynamic World probabilities, we assign a "Bare" label (1) if the probability exceeds 0.30 and a "Vegetated" label (0) if it is below 0.11 (1/9). Pixels with ambiguous probabilities between these thresholds are discarded to ensure high-quality training data.

The models are trained on data from years 2022 and 2023 and evaluated on a strictly held-out test set from 2024 to assess forecasting capability.

Methodology

Problem Formulation

As aforementioned above, we frame land degradation forecasting as a supervised binary classification problem. The goal is to predict the land cover state (Bare vs. Vegetated) of a location in year t+1, given only satellite imagery and climate data available up to year t. This ensures a strict forecasting regime with no temporal data leakage.

Comparative Modeling Approaches

We investigate two distinct paradigms for this task:

  • Random Forest (Feature Engineering): We train a pixel-level classifier on a 58-dimensional vector of handcrafted features. This includes raw Sentinel-2 reflectances, derived spectral indices (NDVI, NDMI, NDBI) to capture vegetation and moisture, and multi-year precipitation statistics from TerraClimate.
  • SatMAE (Representation Learning): We employ a Masked Autoencoder Vision Transformer (ViT) operating at the chip level. The model takes a sequence of temporal Sentinel-2 RGB composites (224x224 pixels) and a precipitation map as input, learning spatiotemporal representations directly from the raw data without manual index calculation.

Results

1. Kubuqi: A Deterministic System

In the Kubuqi dune field, the test year 2024 is heavily dominated by bare soil (positive rate ≈ 0.789). In this setting, the Sentinel-2 only Random Forest achieved exceptional performance, reaching a test accuracy of 0.992 and an AUROC of 0.999. Similarly, the SatMAE forecaster attained a top-1 accuracy of 98.4% on the held-out test set.

Crucially, adding precipitation features provided zero marginal gain. The forward selection algorithm rejected all climate features, indicating that at 10 m resolution, the future state is almost entirely determined by the current land cover configuration (stable dunes vs. engineered shelter belts).

2. East MN: Policy-Driven Recovery

The East MN region presented a massive shift toward vegetation, with bare pixels constituting only 1.6% of the 2024 test area. Performance was lower here due to this extreme imbalance; the Random Forest achieved an AUROC of 0.616 and Balanced Accuracy of 0.599.

The models excelled at identifying vegetation (Precision ≈ 0.988) but struggled with rare bare patches. Consistent with Kubuqi, adding TerraClimate precipitation improved the AUROC by a negligible 0.002. This suggests the recovery is driven by local human interventions (e.g., grazing management) visible in the imagery, rather than short-term rainfall anomalies.

3. SatMAE Training Dynamics

The SatMAE model showed smooth convergence during fine-tuning. The training loss dropped rapidly in the first 500 steps, while validation accuracy on Kubuqi climbed from a majority baseline of ~90% to a plateau near 98.4% by step 2200.
